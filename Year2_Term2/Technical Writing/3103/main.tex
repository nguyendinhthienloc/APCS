\title{Evaluating XAI Metrics in Medical AI:\\
A Cross-Study Co-occurrence Analysis}
\date{}

\begin{document}

\maketitle

\begin{abstract}
Explainable Artificial Intelligence (XAI) is increasingly required in clinical settings, yet the metrics used to evaluate explanation quality in medical AI remain fragmented and poorly standardised. This paper presents a cross-study co-occurrence analysis of XAI evaluation metrics across an expanded corpus of 52 peer-reviewed studies spanning five medical sub-domains: oncology, cardiology, neurology, radiology, and clinical decision support. Using a four-phase methodology --- corpus collection, criterion extraction and normalisation, cross-domain alignment, and taxonomy construction --- we identify six quantitative metrics in active use and examine their co-occurrence patterns. Faithfulness dominates but is no longer universal, appearing in 47 of 52 studies (90\%), with its absence concentrated in human-centred evaluation studies --- a pattern that itself reveals a structural gap between technical and clinician-oriented XAI research. Sensitivity and Faithfulness co-occur in 33 studies (63\%), while AOPC/MoRF and Pixel Flipping form the most redundant pair ($\rho = 0.51$, $p < 0.001$). Sufficiency remains isolated and underutilised despite its direct clinical relevance. Mapping the corpus onto a three-layer taxonomy of mathematical faithfulness, system robustness, and human-centred trust reveals that evaluation effort remains heavily concentrated in the first layer, with robustness addressed in 18 studies (35\%) and human-centred criteria in only 7 (13\%). We propose a minimal but comprehensive evaluation set covering all three layers and call for shared benchmarks and reporting standards, particularly for underserved sub-domains such as neurology.
\end{abstract}
