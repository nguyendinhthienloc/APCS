\section{Results}
% ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

The following sections report the findings of our four-phase methodology applied
to the 52-study corpus, organised by corpus composition, metric co-occurrence,
cross-domain alignment, and taxonomy validation. Interpretive analysis of these
findings is deferred to the Discussion (Section~6).

% ──────────────────────────────────────
\subsection{Corpus Composition and Metric Distribution}

The corpus spans 52 evaluation studies distributed across five medical
sub-domains: oncology ($n = 9$), radiology ($n = 12$), cardiology ($n = 9$),
neuroimaging ($n = 7$), and clinical decision support ($n =
15$).\footnote{Throughout this analysis, studies categorised under Clinical AI,
Clinical ML, Healthcare ML, and Medical Imaging are grouped under the
``Clinical'' label for cross-domain comparison. This grouping consolidates
studies that address general clinical evaluation methodology or span multiple
imaging modalities without being specific to a single anatomical sub-domain.}
Imaging-heavy applications contribute the largest share.
Table~\ref{tab:dataset_papers} provides a structured summary of the
studies included in the corpus.

Across all studies, six quantitative evaluation metrics were identified:
Faithfulness, Sensitivity, AOPC/MoRF, Pixel Flipping, IROF, and Sufficiency.
Figure~\ref{fig:metric_frequency} presents the distribution of these metrics
across the corpus. Faithfulness was the most frequently reported metric,
appearing in 47 of 52 studies (90.4\%), followed by Sensitivity in 34 studies
(65.4\%), Pixel Flipping in 14 studies (26.9\%), and AOPC/MoRF in 13 studies
(25.0\%). IROF and Sufficiency each appeared in 4 studies (7.7\%).

The five studies in which Faithfulness was absent comprise three human-centred
evaluation
designs~\cite{rezaeian2026explainability,finzel2024human,bauer2025human} and
two consistency-focused
benchmarks~\cite{qin2025consistency,alpsalaz2026explainability}. The
concentration of Faithfulness absence in these study types is addressed in
the Discussion.

\subsection{Metric Co-occurrence and Correlation}

Pairwise co-occurrence analysis revealed three distinct patterns, as
illustrated in Figures~\ref{fig:pairwise_cooccurrence}
and~\ref{fig:cooccurrence_heatmap}.

First, Faithfulness and Sensitivity co-occurred in 33 of the 47 studies
reporting Faithfulness (70.2\% of Faithfulness-reporting studies; 63.5\% of
the full corpus). In the five studies lacking Faithfulness, Sensitivity
appeared in one case~\cite{qin2025consistency}.
Table~\ref{tab:sensitivity_methods} lists the studies in which sensitivity
analysis formed a primary evaluation method.

Second, AOPC/MoRF and Pixel Flipping co-occurred in 7 studies and produced
the strongest Spearman correlation in the corpus ($\rho = 0.51$, $p < 0.001$).
AOPC/MoRF and IROF also exhibited a statistically significant correlation
($\rho = 0.37$, $p = 0.007$). The Faithfulness--Sensitivity correlation was
modest but significant ($\rho = 0.33$, $p = 0.016$).

Third, Sufficiency exhibited negligible co-occurrence with all other metrics
and produced negative correlations ranging from $\rho = -0.09$ to
$\rho = -0.15$ (all non-significant at $\alpha = 0.05$).

\subsection{Cross-Domain Alignment Within Medical Sub-domains}

Comparison of the normalised criterion list across the five medical
sub-domains revealed both cross-domain and domain-specific patterns.
Faithfulness and Sensitivity qualified as near-cross-domain criteria,
appearing in at least four of the five sub-domains with broadly comparable
operationalisations. Table~\ref{tab:faithfulness_metrics} summarises studies
that explicitly report Faithfulness Correlation or Faithfulness Estimate as
their primary evaluation metric.

IROF and Pixel Flipping were predominantly concentrated in radiology and
oncology, where saliency-map explanations are standard practice. The
expanded radiology sub-corpus---including benchmarking studies such as
Saporta et al.~\cite{saporta2022benchmarking} in \textit{Nature Machine
Intelligence} and Wollek et al.~\cite{wollek2023attention} in
\textit{Radiology: AI}---exhibited the highest metric diversity among all
sub-domains. Neuroimaging studies relied predominantly on Sensitivity, with
notably fewer metrics per study than other sub-domains; Qin~\cite{qin2025consistency}
represents an emerging exception, applying consistency and stability
benchmarking to brain MRI classification. Oncology studies were the most
metrically diverse, combining technical and, in several cases, user-study-based
criteria, with contributions such as Cerekci et al.~\cite{cerekci2024quantitative}
providing dedicated quantitative evaluation of saliency methods in mammography.
The clinical sub-domain expanded most substantially, incorporating
human-centred studies~\cite{rezaeian2026explainability,bauer2025human,finzel2024human}
that operationalise Layer~3 trust criteria.

\subsection{Taxonomy Validation Against the Corpus}

Mapping the 52 studies onto the three-layer taxonomy yielded the following
distribution: 47 of 52 studies (90.4\%) reported at least one mathematical
faithfulness criterion (Layer~1); 18 studies (34.6\%) addressed system
robustness through multi-metric perturbation analyses (Layer~2); and 7
studies (13.5\%) included any human-centred trust measure or sufficiency
check (Layer~3). Figure~\ref{fig:taxonomy_diagram} illustrates the taxonomy
structure alongside the empirical distribution of studies across layers.

Ihongbe and Fouad~\cite{IhongbeFouad2024}, Fouad et al.~\cite{11355720},
Rezaeian et al.~\cite{rezaeian2026explainability}, Bauer and
Michalowski~\cite{bauer2025human}, and Finzel~\cite{finzel2024human}
represent the most explicit operationalisations of the trust layer,
collectively demonstrating that cognitive load, clinician comprehension, and
diagnostic confidence are measurable constructs.

Table~\ref{tab:perturbation_methods} lists perturbation-based and
pixel-flipping studies, which collectively represent the most common
operationalisation of the faithfulness layer in medical imaging contexts.
Cross-layer criteria, most notably contrastive explanations, appeared in
several studies and were flagged accordingly. Among studies employing Grad-CAM
as the primary explanation method, Faithfulness scores tended to be higher
in several sub-domains, although a systematic comparative evaluation of
explanation methods was outside the scope of this review.
